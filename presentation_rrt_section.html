Rapidly Exploring Random Trees (RRT)

RRT Algorithm:

1 Initialize search tree with xstart
2 while stopping criterion is not met
3 sample xsample ∼ 𝒳   (uniform random point in free space, or biased toward known frontiers)
4 find xnearest — closest node in tree to xsample
5 steer from xnearest toward xsample by step size δ → obtain xnew
6 if xnew and the edge xnearest–xnew are collision-free
7 add xnew to tree with edge from xnearest
8 if xnew ∈ 𝒳goal  return SUCCESS
9 return FAILURE
In our implementation:   δ = 0.50 m  |  max iterations = 1000  |  robot inflation radius = 5 cells (= 0.25 m clearance)  |  30% of samples are biased toward already-detected frontier cells to accelerate convergence. The goal region is not a fixed target but the nearest frontier — the boundary between observed free space and unknown space in the SLAM occupancy grid.
RRT for Frontier-Based Exploration

From path planning to autonomous exploration

Standard RRT finds a path from a fixed start to a known goal. In our escape-room scenario, there is no pre-defined goal — the robot must discover the room by itself. We adapt RRT to do this by replacing the fixed goal region with frontier cells: grid cells that sit on the boundary between observed free space and unexplored (unknown) space.

Every 3 seconds the planner timer fires, takes a snapshot of the current SLAM occupancy grid, inflates obstacles by the robot radius, runs RRT from the robot's estimated position, and selects the frontier that scores best on a combined distance + heading-alignment cost. The resulting path is published on /exploration_path and the goal marker on /rrt_goal — both visible in RViz.

Frontier scoring

  • Frontiers too close (< 0.75 m) are rejected — they may be inside the inflation buffer.
  • Frontiers too far (> 1.35 m) are rejected — they may not be reachable in a single RRT run.
  • Among the remaining candidates the cost is:   |dist − 1.0 m|  +  (turn_angle / π) × 0.25   so the planner slightly prefers frontiers roughly ahead of the robot.
  • Before accepting the best frontier, the path is re-checked against a fresh map snapshot to discard any goal that has since become occupied.
Live Demo — Two Configurations Explained

Why we run two separate programs

Getting a robot to follow RRT paths reliably in a real room requires that every layer in the stack works well at the same time: the SLAM pose must be accurate, odometry must not drift, the occupancy grid must reflect real walls, and the path follower must track curved waypoint sequences without overshooting or orbiting. In our current state of development not all of these are simultaneously reliable — so we split the demo into two focused parts to show what is working.

Demo 1  —  main.py

RRT planning & visualisation + Safe LiDAR motion

  • The RRT planner runs every 3 s, builds a frontier path and publishes it to RViz (/exploration_path, /rrt_goal).
  • The robot's physical motion is handled entirely by SafeLidarMotion — a reactive controller that reads LiDAR sectors and drives forward while steering away from obstacles.
  • The path-follower and control timer inside rrt_exploration.py are commented out; RRT produces plans for visualisation only and never sends a /cmd_vel command.
  • The robot roams freely and safely around the room while the audience can see, in RViz, the tree expanding toward unmapped areas in real time.
Demo 2  —  main2.py

RRT planning + waypoint follower ("my rrt", commit 9686606)

  • Uses rrt_explorer2.py — an earlier, simpler version of the explorer that also drives the robot via a two-phase waypoint follower: TURN in place until aligned, then DRIVE straight to the next waypoint.
  • No SafeLidarMotion is running — the RRT explorer is the sole source of /cmd_vel commands.
  • This is the more ambitious but less stable version: SLAM drift, timing mismatches between the planner and the controller, and the lack of local obstacle avoidance mean the robot can veer off-path and sometimes collide with walls.
  • Shown as a work-in-progress to demonstrate the intended end-to-end pipeline.
Why Path Following Is Difficult on Real Hardware

The gap between simulation and reality

On paper, a two-phase follower — turn to face the waypoint, then drive straight — should work well. In practice, several things go wrong simultaneously on the physical robot:

  • SLAM pose latency. The EKF SLAM update runs after each LiDAR scan, but there is a small lag between the robot's true position and the published /slam_pose. When the follower reads the pose to compute the heading error, the robot has already moved slightly — causing the controller to overshoot turns.
  • Odometry drift. The omnidirectional wheels produce wheel-slip, especially during in-place rotation. This means the raw odometry (which feeds the EKF prediction step) gradually disagrees with the LiDAR-corrected pose, and the follower may think the robot is aligned when it is not.
  • No local obstacle avoidance in the follower. RRT plans a collision-free path at planning time, but the map is continuously updated. By the time the robot reaches a waypoint, a wall that was previously mapped at low confidence may now be solidly marked as occupied — but the follower keeps trying to reach the waypoint and drives straight into it.
  • Path re-planning lag. The planner runs every 3 seconds. If an obstacle appears between two planner ticks, the robot has no way to know about it until the next plan is ready, and there is nothing to prevent a collision in the meantime.

Why Demo 1 is safer

SafeLidarMotion runs at 10 Hz and reacts to the current LiDAR reading directly, with no dependence on SLAM pose accuracy or map quality. It does not know where it is going — but it never hits a wall. Combining this reactive layer with the RRT planner gives us a robot that moves safely through the environment while the planning layer demonstrates correct frontier detection and path construction in RViz. This separation is a valid and commonly used architecture in mobile robotics — it corresponds to the distinction between the global planner (RRT, runs slowly on the map) and the local planner (reactive, runs fast on raw sensor data).
